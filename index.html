Textual Entailment

Textual Entailment is the NLP task of determining whether the meaning of a given text passage entails that of another. Textual Entailment is a fundamental problem in natural language understanding which requires the ability to abstract over the inherent syntactic and semantic variability in natural language.

My work for CCG is based on the Textual Entailment Task in Natural Language Processing (NLP); I am investigating aspects of numeric and temporal reasoning in regard to this task.

Statistical Relational Learning

Statistical Relational Learning (SRL) is an emerging subdiscipline of Machine Learning concerned with building statistical models of domains with relational structure, meaning the domains of interest are composed of both statistically dependent and independent objects, i.e. the typical independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) assumption made in statistics and machine learning is no longer present in relational domains.

The motivations of SRL are many, but boil down to a few pillars:

  • Real-world problems violate the i.i.d. assumption typically made in statistical methods because objects in the world are related.

  • When we don't model the dependence between data instances, we often throw out valuable information. Hence, exploiting relationships amongst objects can lead to greater predictive accuracy in computational models and better understanding of the domain, at the costs of greater modeling, computational, and sample complexity.

  • SRL offers a growth plan for machine learning, allowing researchers to model bigger problems.

The most enticing of these three is definitely the later, there appears to be a heavy shift in the direction of learning structured output: learning is no longer classify single feature vectors, but entire structures. Examples of such domains include hyper-linked document classification, medical diagnosis, semantic role labeling, and social network analysis.

Reinforcement Learning

Reinforcement learning (RL) is a protocol for learning to make decisions within an environment based on rewards and punishment. The objective of these interactions is to learn a policy (i.e., a mapping from states to actions), which maximizes the expected accumulated reward over time. RL distinguishes itself from Supervised Learning in that agents must learn from the consequences of their actions, rather than being explicitly taught. This gives rise to the problem of assigning credit or blame to a single action for the performance of the entire policy.

Although RL techniques have elegant theoretical / mathematical foundations, applying these techniques to problems with large state spaces such as robot control has proven to be quite difficult. Generally, I am interested in taking RL to big domains where the agent cannot, in any reasonable amount of time, explore the entire state space (or even a reasonable fraction of it). An example of such a domain is Tetris (roughly 10^60 states), which was the subject of my undergraduate thesis. What I find interesting about Tetris is that despite it's enormous state space, humans can play the game very well. I believe this is because they can establish important high-level concepts, such as, a line or a hole, from only playing the game for a few minutes. Thus, in order to create an effective Tetris agent it is necessary to inform the agent of these concepts rather than have the agent learn to play Tetris from a blank slate.

I am also interested in applications of reinforcement learning which require agents to generalize what they have learned from one task to a new one which is very similar. In most applications of reinforcement learning, policies learned are very specific to a single task and cannot be applied to a new task no matter how similar it may be. Currently, I am working on having a simulated aircraft do skywriting. Skywriting poses an interesting challenge: the airplane controller/planner clearly cannot train on all possibly phrases which it will eventually see -- How can we break the problem up into smaller pieces and then combine them in a fashion which will allow the the airplane to write anything? Simply learning each letter independently is one option, but is inefficient and does not in anyway aid us in learning to write in Chinese.

Routerbot

A Routerbot is a robot which uses a Wifi router (Linksys WRT54GL) as both an communication means and an onboard computer. Many people do not know that a router is actually a simple computer capable of running an operating system, executing programs, and serial port communication. Thanks to Andrey Mikhalchuk's tutorial, the project is well under way. Those interested in Routerbots should check out JBProjects's take on the idea.

Reasoning About Quantities in Natural Language

On a daily basis, we communicate the notions of money, time, distance, temperature, severity, reliability, etc. via written expressions of quantitative information. However, little work from the Natural Language Processing community has targeted the role of quantities in Natural Language Understanding. In this project, we formulate and analyze a new task of Quantity Entailment and describe our system, which we show to successfully recognize textual expressions of quantity, represent them in a way that supports reasoning, and perform limited types of reasoning. Check out the web demo.

Event Search Engine

Event (vertical) search engine built in 4 weeks for the Multimodal Information Access and Synthesis (MIAS) Summer Institute research projects. This system aggregates, de-duplicates, indexes, and provides a novel search interface to events (things to do) in the Champaign-Urbana, IL area. The search interface allows users to ask natural-language-like queries, such as "free concerts near green & lincoln" or "anything for all ages next week".
